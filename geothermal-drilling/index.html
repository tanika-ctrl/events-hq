01 / Foundation

What Is Geothermal Energy?

The word "geothermal" comes from the Greek geo (Earth) and therme (heat). Earth's interior reaches temperatures exceeding 5,000°C at its core — comparable to the surface of the Sun.

🌍

Formation Heat

Residual heat from Earth's violent formation 4.5 billion years ago, slowly radiating outward from the core.

⚛️

Radioactive Decay

Ongoing heat from the decay of isotopes — uranium-235, thorium-232, and potassium-40 — deep in the mantle and crust.

🏔️

Plate Tectonics

Heat transferred via the movement of tectonic plates, concentrating in volcanic zones and rift valleys.

100°C+ per km in volcanically active zones

02 / Process

Drilling Through Kilometers of Rock

A single geothermal well can take 60–90 days and cost $5–10 million. Every meter drilled is a carefully engineered feat against extreme heat and pressure.

Depth 0 m
Temp 15°C
STEP 01

Site Assessment

Before a single drill bit touches rock, extensive surveys map the subsurface. Seismic surveys reveal underground structures, magnetotelluric (MT) surveys detect electrical resistivity, and satellite thermal imaging identifies heat anomalies.

Duration 6–24 months
Cost $500K–$2M
Key tool MT Survey
STEP 02

Surface Preparation & Conductor Casing

The wellsite is graded and equipped. A large-diameter conductor casing (18–36 inches) is driven or drilled into the ground to 30–60m depth, providing structural support for the wellhead and preventing surface collapse.

Depth 0–60m
Casing size 18–36"
STEP 03

Rotary Drilling (Surface Hole)

A rotary drill rig drives a large-diameter bit through the first 200–600m. Drilling mud — a specialized fluid mixture — circulates down the drill string, cools the bit, carries rock cuttings to the surface, and maintains wellbore pressure.

Depth 60–600m
Rate 3–15m/hr
Bit size 17.5"
STEP 04

Intermediate Casing & Cementing

After each major section is drilled, steel casing is lowered and cemented in place. Cementing fills the gap between the casing and rock, isolating fresh water zones, preventing blowouts, and providing structural integrity against high-temperature steam.

Depth 600–1,500m
Casing size 9.5–13.375"
STEP 05

Production Drilling & Directional Control

The production section drills into the target reservoir. Directional drilling steers the wellbore at angles, allowing a single surface pad to access multiple subsurface targets. Measurement-While-Drilling (MWD) tools transmit real-time data on temperature, pressure, and formation properties.

Depth 1.5–3km+
Temp encountered 150–300°C
STEP 06

Well Testing & Completion

Once target depth is reached, the well is tested for flow rate, temperature, and pressure. A wellhead and production casing are installed. A successful well might produce 5–50 kg/s of steam or hot water, enough for 1–10 MW of electrical capacity.

Flow rate 5–50 kg/s
Capacity 1–10 MW/well
Success rate 60–80%

Binary Cycle Plants

Works with lower-temperature resources. Geothermal fluid never contacts the turbine. Instead, it passes through a heat exchanger, transferring its heat to a secondary "working fluid" (isobutane or isopentane) with a lower boiling point. This fluid vaporizes and drives the turbine in a completely closed loop.

The Future Deep Hot Rock

Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS)

EGS creates artificial geothermal reservoirs in dry hot rock where no natural water exists. By drilling 3–10km and hydraulically fracturing the rock, water can be circulated through the newly created fracture network, extracting heat from virtually anywhere on Earth.

04 / Technology

The Machines That Go Miles Deep

Geothermal drilling pushes oil & gas technology to its extremes — combining extreme heat resistance with precision engineering at depths of 3,000+ meters.

+

Rotary Drill String

A continuous string of hollow steel pipes, each 9–10m long, that rotates the drill bit and transmits drilling fluid. Can extend 3+ km with hundreds of pipe sections.

The drill string must withstand enormous rotational torque, compression from its own weight, and temperatures exceeding 300°C at the bit. High-chrome alloy steels and special thread compounds prevent galling at connections.
+

PDC & Tricone Bits

Polycrystalline Diamond Compact (PDC) bits use synthetic diamond cutters on a steel body. Tricone bits have three rotating cones with tungsten carbide teeth for hard, abrasive formations.

In geothermal, PDC bits can drill 500–1,000m before replacement. At extreme depths, bit temperature can exceed 250°C. Thermal stability of the diamond cutters is a critical engineering challenge unique to geothermal drilling.
+

Steel Casing

Hollow steel pipe sections cemented permanently into the wellbore. Each casing string isolates zones, prevents collapse, and provides a sealed conduit for fluid flow.

A typical geothermal well uses 3–4 nested casing strings, each progressively smaller in diameter — 30", 20", 13.375", down to 9.625" production casing. Special H2S-resistant steel grades are used in corrosive environments.
+

Drilling Fluid (Mud)

A carefully engineered mixture of water, bentonite clay, and chemical additives pumped down the drill string. Cools the bit, carries cuttings to surface, and maintains wellbore pressure.

In geothermal wells, standard mud systems can fail at temperatures above 150°C. High-temperature muds use synthetic polymers, potassium-based fluids, or aerated systems. The fluid system is continuously monitored and adjusted throughout drilling.
+

Blowout Preventer (BOP)

The primary safety device mounted at the wellhead. A series of hydraulically-actuated rams and annular preventers can seal the well in seconds if high-pressure steam or gas threatens to erupt uncontrolled.

Geothermal BOPs must handle steam at 250°C+ and pressures of 1,000+ psi. Unlike oil well BOPs designed for liquid hydrocarbons, geothermal BOPs require special elastomer seals that resist both high temperature and corrosive geothermal brines.
+

Measurement While Drilling (MWD)

Electronic sensors embedded near the drill bit transmit real-time data — inclination, azimuth, temperature, pressure, and formation resistivity — to the surface via mud pulse or electromagnetic telemetry.

High-temperature MWD tools are a frontier of geothermal technology. Standard electronics fail above 175°C. Silicon carbide semiconductors and hermetically-sealed high-temp electronics are enabling sensors to survive 300°C+ environments that were impossible a decade ago.

Clean, Responsible, Sustainable

Geothermal is one of the cleanest energy sources available — but like all energy, responsible development requires understanding both its advantages and its impacts.

Considerations

Responsible Management

🌋

Induced Seismicity

Fluid injection can trigger micro-seismic events. Modern traffic light protocols automatically pause operations when events exceed M2.0. Careful site selection and injection monitoring have made this manageable in most projects. Notable exception: Basel, Switzerland (2006 project suspended after M3.4).

🏔️

Land Subsidence

Extracting large volumes of fluid can cause ground settling. Modern reinjection practices return 95%+ of extracted fluid to the reservoir, nearly eliminating this risk. Wairakei, New Zealand — an early site that did not reinject — experienced measurable subsidence in the 1970s, providing important lessons.

🌬️

Hydrogen Sulfide (H₂S)

Some reservoirs release H₂S (the "rotten egg" smell). Abatement systems at the plant capture and neutralize emissions. Iceland processes H₂S gas into sulfuric acid, turning a waste product into a commercial commodity.

💧

Water Consumption

Flash steam plants lose water through cooling tower evaporation. Binary cycle plants are near-zero net water consumers. Compared to coal or nuclear plants, geothermal uses 2–10× less water per kWh generated.

Country Case Studies

🇮🇸
Iceland
66% of total energy

Sitting atop the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, Iceland uses geothermal for 65% of space heating and 30% of electricity. The Hellisheiðarvirkjun plant (303 MW electric, 400 MW thermal) is the world's largest combined heat and power geothermal installation.

🇰🇪
Kenya
47% of electricity

The Olkaria complex in Kenya's Rift Valley has grown from 45 MW in 1985 to over 863 MW today. Kenya now exports expertise across Africa, helping Ethiopia and Tanzania develop their own resources.

🇵🇭
Philippines
27% of electricity

The Philippines is the world's third largest geothermal producer, with 1,918 MW across the Leyte, Negros, and Luzon island chains. The Makban complex alone (458 MW) powers over 1 million homes.

🇮🇩
Indonesia
40% of world's reserves

Indonesia holds an estimated 29 GW of geothermal potential — 40% of the world's total — but has only developed 2,277 MW to date. Ambitious government targets aim for 9.3 GW by 2035, requiring 60+ new wells per year.

🇺🇸
United States
3,722 MW installed

The Geysers in California (725 MW) is the world's largest single geothermal complex. Nevada adds another 800+ MW. Next-gen EGS projects by Fervo Energy in Utah are pioneering closed-loop horizontal drilling for geothermal — a potential game changer.

🇳🇿
New Zealand
18% of electricity

Wairakei (1958) was the world's second commercial geothermal plant and pioneered flash steam technology. New Zealand's Taupo Volcanic Zone continues to provide abundant resources, with the Nga Awa Purua plant (140 MW) holding a world record for single-shaft output.

07 / Future

The Geothermal Future

The technology exists. The resources are global. What's changing is the economics, the engineering, and the political will.

2024–25

EGS Demonstration Era

Fervo Energy's Utah project delivers first commercial EGS electricity. Next-gen drill bits reduce well costs by 30%. 1.5 GW of new capacity added globally. DOE "Enhanced Geothermal Shot" program accelerates.

High confidence
2026–28

Cost Breakthrough

DOE target: $45/MWh geothermal (from ~$80/MWh today). First commercial-scale EGS plants online. AI-optimized drilling planning reduces well costs by 40–50%. Global capacity crosses 20 GW.

High confidence
2030

Closed-Loop Goes Commercial

Closed-loop geothermal (circulating fluid in sealed pipes — no fracking required) becomes commercially viable, eliminating induced seismicity concerns. Estimated 60 GW globally installed. Direct lithium extraction from geothermal brine becomes standard practice.

Medium confidence
2035

Deep Drilling Revolution

New drilling technologies (plasma, millimeter-wave energy, laser) reach 15km commercially. Geothermal becomes cost-competitive everywhere without subsidies. Co-production of lithium and other critical minerals from brine creates a second revenue stream.

Medium confidence
2050

Geothermal Everywhere

Potential: 300+ GW globally. EGS could supply 10%+ of world electricity. With sufficiently deep drilling (10+ km), virtually every location on Earth has access to geothermal energy. "Energy independence" becomes achievable for most nations.

Speculative

Geothermal is ready. Are we?

The technology exists. The resources are global. The economics are improving rapidly. What's needed is investment, policy support, and public awareness — the same ingredients that drove the solar and wind revolutions.
